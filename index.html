Steve Leve

Thirty years of building things. These are the stories that don't fit on a resume.

My other sites will happily tell you what I can do for your company. This one is for curious technologists. It's a field journal of the odd, the obsolete, and the formative: laser holograms, touring stage rigs, shrink-wrapped CD-ROMs, duct-tape automation, a 24-minute disaster recovery, and the agent teams I build with today. Old tech is never wasted tech — every era here taught me something I still use.

Analog Origins · 1988–1996

Before software, there was light, sound, and dice. Everything I know about production discipline, systems thinking, and shipping under a hard deadline started here — none of it involved a compiler.

The Holography Lab

Lab assistant in my high school's photography and holography lab. Yes, holography. In a small-town Illinois high school.

  • Lasers, darkrooms, and interference patterns before I ever touched a debugger
  • First lesson in precision: a hologram exposure fails if the table vibrates a micron
  • Where "tinker until it works, then understand why" became a lifelong method

Load In, Run, Strike

WESTEC technical services at Western Illinois University: sound, lights, and stagecraft for touring professional shows, alongside IATSE union crews.

  • The show opens at 8:00 whether your rig is ready or not — my first immovable production deadline
  • Unload, set, run, strike, load: the original deploy pipeline, measured in road cases
  • Live ops before I knew the term: no rollbacks when the curtain is up

Dice & Worldbuilding

Consultant game designer at Lupine Entertainment, shaping mechanics and setting for tabletop role-playing games.

  • Balancing game mechanics is systems design with immediate, brutal playtester feedback
  • Learned that rules and lore have to reinforce each other — architecture and product in miniature
  • The itch for game design and interactive experiences never left

Shrink-Wrapped Software · 1997–1999

Before broadband, software reached people in boxes. At Aztech New Media, we shipped themed "cubes" of 3–7 CD-ROMs to retail shelves. It was distribution engineering for a world that was about to vanish — and the best apprenticeship I could have asked for.

Software by the Cube

As a "New Media Developer" on the four-person Cube Team, I curated demo and shareware collections for retail distribution.

  • Go-to-market meant literal shelf space in major retail markets
  • Curation, licensing, QA, and gold-master deadlines — no patching after ship
  • A business model the internet would erase, and a front-row seat to watching it happen

The Launcher That Configured Itself

Our Delphi III launcher required code changes for every product release — until I moved it to XML configuration files.

  • From copy–paste–change coding to config-only releases: zero code changes between products
  • Dramatically simplified QA and became my first "solve the class of problem" win
  • The seed of a 25-year habit: build the system that builds the thing

Zerglings & Empires

Our Nexus Transmedia division built original games and licensed add-ons for StarCraft and Age of Empires. I got to orbit that work.

  • Organized external beta testing programs before "community management" existed
  • Mentored by engineers building customizable game controller drivers and games
  • Learned how much craft hides beneath a polished title screen

The Web Takes Over · 2001–2008

Agency years: ecommerce, CMS, surveys, and B2B systems for clients in coffee, manufacturing, education, and beyond — plus a side gig that put me on an actual factory floor watching my code do physical work.

Mainframes, Coffee, and Charter Schools

Lead developer at Harp Advertising + Interactive, building client solutions on CFML, ASP.NET, and SQL Server.

  • Bridged a modern B2B parts catalog to a legacy mainframe order system — my first taste of legacy integration
  • Designed a white-label survey platform with a visual designer and branching logic
  • Shipped a 7-site CMS network for Chicago charter schools on DotNetNuke

Duct-Tape Robotics

At Bean Products, a yoga-supply and pillow manufacturer, I automated order fulfillment with VBA and VB6 — and watched it work from the production floor.

  • Email orders → MYOB accounting → UPS WorldShip, orchestrated by Office macros
  • Fewer errors, faster throughput, cheaper shipping — and office staff freed for better work
  • Unforgettable lesson: automation is about people, and it helps to watch your code land

One Platform, Seventeen Years · 2008–2025

I joined ShareASale as the 10th employee and 2nd engineering hire. Three engineers built and scaled an affiliate network to $1B+ in annual transactions. The resume version covers the features and migrations. This is the folklore.

DevOps Before It Had a Name

No ops team, no frameworks, no budget for tools — so we built our own, from first principles.

  • A homegrown deploy tool with rollbacks measured in seconds, years before CI/CD was standard
  • Active-passive regional failover driven by synthetic health checks — entirely DIY
  • Exception logs and threshold alerts wired straight to the three people who could fix them

The 24-Minute Recovery

March 2018, weeks after the founding CTO left and I inherited the platform: the data center's triple-redundant power failed. So did everything behind it.

  • Backup power didn't engage, batteries were past due, and the automated failover's API credentials had expired with the departing CTO
  • Manually executed DNS failover to our Texas DR site and recovered RAID volumes on the primary database server without data loss
  • Full service restored in 24 minutes — the most educational half hour of my career

Now: Systems That Build Systems

Today I build products with agent teams — coding-agent harnesses, agent memory, multi-agent workflows, and the knowledge architecture that keeps them honest. It's the same instinct as that XML launcher in 1998: don't just solve the problem, build the system that solves the class of problem. The tools finally caught up to the idea. Expect writeups here on harness engineering, agent memory experiments, and whatever else I'm tinkering with.
